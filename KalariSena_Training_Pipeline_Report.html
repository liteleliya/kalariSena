3. RL Stage A – Motion Imitation

Problem Formulation

The imitation problem is modeled as a Markov Decision Process

\[ \mathcal{M}=(\mathcal{S},\mathcal{A},P,R,\gamma) \]

where the policy receives the current robot state together with a reference motion and predicts the next robot action.

Observation

\[ o_t=\{q_t,\dot q_t,x^{root}_t,R^{root}_t,q^{ref}_t,\dot q^{ref}_t\} \]

  • q: joint positions
  • \(\dot q\): joint velocities
  • Root pose: floating base position and orientation
  • Reference state: current frame of the NPZ motion

Policy

\[ a_t=\pi_\theta(o_t) \]

Observation
      ↓
 Neural Network
      ↓
 Actor Policy
      ↓
 Joint Actions

Optimization Objective

\[ J(\theta)= \mathbb{E} \left[ \sum_{t=0}^{T} \gamma^t r_t \right] \]

The objective is to maximize the expected discounted reward.

Reward Function

\[ r_t= w_p r_{pose} +w_r r_{root} +w_v r_{vel} +w_c r_{contact} +w_e r_{energy} \]

Velocity\(r_{vel}=e^{-||\dot q-\dot q_{ref}||^2}\)Smooth tracking

4. Future RL Stages

Stage B – Recoverability-aware CoM Control

Instead of only tracking motion, the controller should maintain a recoverable Center of Mass.

\[ CoM=\frac{\sum_i m_i x_i}{\sum_i m_i} \]

\[ r_{CoM}=\exp(-||CoM-CoM_{target}||^2) \]

This reward encourages stable balance during deep stances, kicks and single-leg support.

Stage C – Centroidal Momentum Regulation

Dynamic martial motions generate significant angular momentum.

\[ h=A_G(q)\dot q \]

\[ r_{mom}=-||h-h_{desired}|| \]

This reduces instability during spins, pivots and jumps.

Stage D – Fall Detection and Recovery

Estimate the probability of falling using torso orientation, CoM displacement and angular velocity.

\[ P(fall)>\tau \]

If the threshold is exceeded, switch from the nominal tracking policy to a recovery controller before resuming motion execution.

Stage E – Unified Motion Policy

Instead of training one policy per motion, train a single policy on the complete NPZ motion library.

  • "Rotate and strike with the right leg."

Embedding Generation

Each description is embedded using a Sentence Transformer.

\[ e_i = Encoder(text_i) \]

Motion Retrieval

For an English instruction, compute cosine similarity

\[ sim(e_q,e_i)= \frac{e_q^Te_i} {\|e_q\|\|e_i\|} \]

The NPZ with the highest similarity is retrieved.

6. Suggested Improvement: Motion Library Granularity

Current Challenge

The current Kalarisena videos typically contain long sequences of multiple martial-art motions performed continuously (e.g., running → jump → spinning kick → cartwheel → stance transition). While this preserves the natural choreography of Kalaripayattu, it also introduces several practical challenges during the motion reconstruction and RL training pipeline.

  • Long videos accumulate reconstruction drift over time.
  • Changing camera viewpoints and self-occlusions introduce pose estimation artifacts.
  • Errors in one segment propagate into later segments.
  • Complex transitions often produce noisy root trajectories, foot sliding and contact inconsistencies.
  • The resulting NPZ motions become more difficult for the RL policy to imitate accurately.

Proposed Direction

Inspired by the KungFuBot Athlete Dataset, which is constructed from a large collection of individual motion clips rather than long continuous routines, we propose investigating a more granular motion library for Kalaripayattu.

Instead of treating one long recording as a single training sample, each video can be segmented into a collection of elementary motion primitives.

Each motion primitive can then be independently:

  • retargeted using GEM-X,
  • preprocessed into a clean NPZ trajectory,
  • validated for quality, and
  • added to a unified motion library for RL training.

Potential Advantages

  • Higher-quality motion reconstruction due to shorter clips.
  • Reduced accumulation of retargeting and pose-estimation errors.
  • Cleaner contact estimation and root trajectories.
  • Improved RL convergence through more consistent demonstrations.
  • Easier annotation with natural-language descriptions for the instruction-controlled robot.
  • A richer and more reusable library of atomic Kalaripayattu skills.

Will This Lose the Essence of Kalaripayattu?

Not necessarily. In fact, this can be viewed as a hierarchical representation of Kalaripayattu rather than a simplification of it.

The low-level motion library would consist of high-quality primitive skills (e.g., jump, spinning kick, cartwheel, horse stance, defensive block), while longer Kalaripayattu routines would be represented as ordered sequences of these primitives. This preserves the expressive nature of the martial art while significantly improving the quality of the learned motion representations.

This hierarchical formulation offers the best of both worlds: robust RL training on clean, well-segmented demonstrations, while still enabling the robot to execute complete Kalaripayattu routines by composing multiple learned skills. It also naturally aligns with the future language-controlled framework, where an instruction such as "run forward, perform a spinning kick, then finish in a guard stance" can be decomposed into a sequence of retrieved motion primitives.
